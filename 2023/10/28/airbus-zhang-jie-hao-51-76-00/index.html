51-76-00 - Description - SEALING REPAIRS

51-76-00-描述-密封修理

1.General

A.The use of sealants in repair procedures and the repair of seals, is given in Chapter 51-76-11.

B.The repair of fuel tank sealing is given in Chapter 51-76-12.

C.It is recommended that Chapter 51-24-00, Sealing - General is read before using the information in Chapter 51-76-11 and Chapter 51-76-12.

2.Terms and Definitions

A.Table 1, below shows the terms, used in sealant procedures and their appropriate definitions.

TERM DEFINITION
Application Life<1> This is the period of time that is often referred to as ‘Pot Life’ and is the time that a sealant remains suitable for application after it has been mixed. Barrier coating a layer of quick-drying sealant is applied over a layer of slow-drying sealant before this sealant has fully cured. This is usually done to prevent swarf and other repair debris sticking to the original sealant, this allows the repair work to continue, if necessary, after a short time.
Protective Coating A protective coating may be applied to protect the sealant in service.
Breakable Joint A joint that can be easily disassembled.
Cure This refers to the change of a sealant from its original mixed condition to its final condition. This is usually caused by a chemical action when the two parts of a sealant are mixed together.
Fillet Referred to as a bead and is formed when sealant is applied into gaps or corners in the structure. The bead can be formed from sealant that is applied for this purpose, or from the interfay sealant that is squeezed out when a joint is assembled.
Initial Cure Time <1> This is the minimum period of time required before a sealant is cured enough to permit a sealed joint to be pressure tested. Corresponding hardness is defined in each product Technical Data Sheet (TDS).
Interfay Sealant A layer of sealant applied to the contacting (faying) surfaces of repair parts before they are assembled.
Leak Path This is the path along which a fluid, for example, fuel or water, can get out of or into a sealed area.
Overcoating This term refers to the application of a brushing thickness sealant over a joint.
Tack Free Time <1> This is the period of time measured from when the sealant is mixed until its surface should be “touch dry”.
Work Life <1> This is the period of time within which a joint that must be sealed, must be riveted, bolted or temporarily fastened, after the sealant has been applied to the contacting surfaces of the joint.
Adhesion Promoter A material which may be used in conjunction with a sealant as an aid to adhesion on clean surface.

Table 1 - Terms and Definitions

<1>The actual times that are applicable to the terms are given in the manufacturer’s data sheets. Work life, application life, cure time and tack free time vary as a function of the temperature and of the relative humidity. Baseline temperature is 23° C and 50 +/- 5% hygrometry. Increasing the relative humidity reduces application, tack-free and curing time. Reducing relative humidity will increase these times. The same trend is observed with the temperature.

3.Sealing Repair Materials

A.Sealing material has to be selected depending on the use and area. The Consumable Material List (CML) gives the correspondence between the CML application code and the alternative products reference with necessary information from supplier.

Sealing products are supplied as two parts sets comprising of a base compound and an accelerator or premixed and frozen material (with limited shelf life).

An adhesion promoter may be required depending on the used sealant. The CML is providing the adhesion promoter reference if required (given in the comments) with the selected sealant.

B.Repair Material Uses

(1)Sealant

Sealants are available with various viscosities and application times in hours noted as class.

The maximum application time in hours is indicated for each sealant by a dash number (e.g. ½ indicates a half hour application time). This application time is in standard temperature and hygrometry conditions.

The three following classes correspond to:

(a)Slightly fluid product applied by brush, syringe gun or bell. Usually used for fillet and fasteners overcoating.

(b)Thick product applied by spatula, extrusion gun, roller or syringe. Used for fillet bead and filling of cavities.

(c)Slightly fluid product, an aerobic polymerisation applied by brush, roller or spatula. Used for interfay and fastener wet installation.

(2)Adhesion Promoter

Adhesion promoter is applied by brushing or wiping. Excess of adhesion promoter must be removed with a clean lint free cloth.

Application of adhesion promoter in unprotected titanium may require specific care such as titanium part that must be at normal ambient temperature.

C.Mixing a Sealant and Sealant Preparation

You must only mix the two parts of matched base and curing agent together (check that batch numbers on both containers correspond). All equipment used in mixing and handling sealants must be clean and must not contaminate sealants such as wax coatings.

One of the following methods can be used.

(1)Manual Mixing

You must put the entire accelerator into the base compound and mix them together well. Do this until the color of the mixture is same. Try not to make air bubbles in the mixture. Do not deviate from mixing ratio given by manufacturer when using sealant supplied in bulk.

Short application life 1/6, 1/2 must only be mixed in small batches and close to the work area, check also the temperature and humidity.

(2)Cartridge Mixing

Pre-measured cartridges must be mixed manually or by cartridge mixers in accordance with the manufacturer’s instructions for the time required to produce homogeneous mix.

(3)Storage of Sealant

(a)Storage of unmixed sealant

Sealant must be stored in unopened container between 10° C (50° F) to 27° C (80.6° F). Adhesion promoters are sensitive to moisture so container must be closed after each use.

(b)Storage of mixed and frozen tubes

It is forbidden to freeze thawed frozen sealant.

Refrigeration of mixed sealants must only be performed with sealants having an application life of 2 hours or more.

(c)Thawing of refrigerated sealants

Frozen sealants can be defrosted in heated water bath at 50° C (122° F) +/− 2° C (35.6° F) (thawing time around 4 minutes) or at ambient temperature (thawing time around 30 minutes). The core of the container must reach 16° C (60.8° F) (between 15° C (59° F) to 25° C (77° F)) before sealant application can start.

(d)Defrosting in microwave oven

Microwave equipment with power regulator, digital timer with +/− 0.5 minutes measuring accuracy, temperature control with +/− 2° C (35.6° F) accuracy, power level on the order of 650−700W.

A microwave oven can be used for sealant thawing (except syringes and 20 gram cups) providing it is not used for thawing other items such as food. The optimum defrost cycle must be established experimentally for each sealing compound, type of packing and quantity. The defrost cycle must be an interrupted cycle (i.e. 5s heat, 15s of soak), the number of cycles corresponding to the time required for the temperature at the core of sealant to reach a temperature between 15° C (59° F) to 25° C (77° F) (after 5 minutes homogenisation at room temperature, for cups you can mix the compound). The defrost cycle may vary from one oven to another. Microwaves can produce hot spots in the sealant therefore controls must be placed to ensure that the temperature of the defrosted sealant does not exceed 35° C (95° F) at any point of the container.

The containers must be placed on the edge of the turning plate never in its centre.

Only packages of the same type filled up with equal quantity, the same class of material and with the same life time can be defrosted in one same operation.

Defrosting of deep−frozen sealants reduce the application time by up to 45 minutes.

Sealant must never be defrosted in the microwave oven at full power. To ensure repeatability and temperature uniformity use low power setting i.e. 100W.

D.Sealant Application

(1)Environmental Condition

(a)You must not apply the sealant when the structure temperature is less than 10° C (50° F) or more than 50° C (122° F). The recommended conditions for mixing and application of sealants are:

Temperature 15° C (59° F) − 35° C (95° F), relative humidity 10−80% (Refer to Figure 001).

Figure 001 / 51-76-00 (sheet 1) Environmental Condition for Sealant Repair

(2)Sealant Curing

(a)Terminology

Application life is the time the mixed sealant remains at a consistency suitable for application. It is the time available for the sealant to wet the surface.

For fillet and overcoat sealants it is the maximum period of time within which sealant must be tooled into shape work life (assembly time, squeeze−out time, squeeze−out life).

For interfay sealants it is the period of time after mixing within which an interfay joint must be fastened at the required torque using permanent or temporary fasteners.

Tack free time is the time required for a curing sealant to loose surface tackiness or stickiness.

Cure time is the period of time after mixing that is required for the sealant to achieve a minimum hardness value of 35 shore A for polysulfide or 30 shore A for polythioether.

(b)Curing

Life of sealant is valid for a given temperature and humidity. It depends on sealant. Please refer to the manufacturer TDS for accurate information.

Sealant curing can be accelerated by heating after solvent flash off but not higher than 50° C (122° F). Refer to quick curing device for accelerating the sealant curing (Refer to Chapter 51-76-12). Above 50° C (122° F) bubbles will occur in sealant.

(c)Quick curing sealant device

Devices such as hot air blowers, infra-red lamps and thermoreactor technology, which uses catalytic combustion can be used.

(d)Protective barrier coating

Product as per CML application code 06HAA1 applied on top of the sealant as protective overcoat or barrier coatings may be used as an aid to prevent damage or contamination of sealant which has not fully cured.

A barrier coating is applied on sealant after the sealant is tack free. Make sure that the barrier coat overlaps existing sealant around the repair area. If a second coat is applied, wait 20 minutes between layers.

This coating is not resistant to phosphate ester hydraulic fluid and will therefore only provide temporary protection. This method is not recommended for application in fuel tank to accelerate refuelling.

The preferred method is to use quick cure sealant or accelerate the cure cycle.
